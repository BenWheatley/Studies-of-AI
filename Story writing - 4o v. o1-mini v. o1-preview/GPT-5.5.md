Write chapter 1 of an HFY story, but instead of focussing on human physicality, focus on the mind. Most of the interplanetary civilisations are much more cooperative than humans are and found it easy to agree to treaties that require limited growth; most of the species known to the interplanetary civilisations are even more cooperative and never developed space travel at all due to the easy path to space being via weapons of war which they never needed to invent. The one known space-faring species that is more aggressive than humans, spends most of its time fighting itself rather than expanding, though forces from them occasionally terrorise the rest of the galactic community.

## Chapter 1 — The Quiet Species

The Assembly habitat rotated with mathematically perfect restraint.

Not because the engineers lacked ambition, but because excessive gravity gradients increased maintenance fatigue by 0.03% over a century, and no species within the Compact considered such waste acceptable.

Ambassador Thir felt the familiar satisfaction of entering a system that behaved exactly as designed. The corridor lighting brightened ahead of him in anticipation of his movement, then dimmed behind him to conserve energy. No sound except airflow. No unnecessary ornamentation. No territorial displays.

Civilisation.

The chamber doors opened before he reached them.

Thirty-seven delegates waited within concentric rings of pale seating grown from living calcite. Conversations ceased as he entered—not out of hostility, but courtesy. Interrupting another mind's concentration was considered a primitive impulse among Compact species.

Thir settled into his cradle-platform and folded his manipulators beneath his mantle.

At the chamber's centre, Archivist Kel projected the image again.

A blue world.

Cloud-heavy. Wide oceans. Artificial satellites cluttering near orbit like metal spores.

Earth.

The silence persisted longer than protocol required.

Finally, Representative Ool spoke from the aquatic delegation pool.

“We have verified the transmission origin?”

Kel inclined her cranial fronds. “Repeatedly.”

“And the casualty estimates?”

“Still incomplete.”

A pulse of muted distress travelled through the chamber. Not panic. Compact species did not panic easily. Panic was an evolutionary adaptation associated with unstable social structures and high intra-species violence rates.

Humanity, according to the dossier, possessed both in abundance.

Kel expanded the projection.

Not cities. Not infrastructure.

Battlefields.

Missile trails crossing continents. Oceanic carrier groups. Ruined urban zones. Primitive kinetic firearms. Atmospheric aircraft designed solely to destroy other aircraft. Thousands of recorded conflicts across barely two centuries of electronic archival history.

One clip showed two human soldiers dragging a wounded comrade through incoming fire.

Another showed medics from opposing armies treating civilians together during a ceasefire that lasted less than a day.

Then another showed the same armies shelling each other again before the week ended.

The chamber's distress deepened into incomprehension.

“They cooperate,” whispered Ool, “and compete simultaneously.”

“Impossible,” muttered another delegate.

“No,” said Thir quietly. “Merely expensive.”

Several delegates turned toward him.

Thir activated his console and overlaid comparative evolutionary charts above the battlefield recordings.

“Compact species evolved within narrow behavioural convergence bands,” he said. “Low aggression stabilised early. Disputes became ritualised before industrialisation thresholds. Resource conflicts declined. Technological development continued under consensus structures.”

This was elementary education. Even juveniles knew it.

Yet no one interrupted him, because the humans required new language.

“Our species reached space because cooperation permitted civilisation,” Thir continued. “But cooperation also imposed limits. Population treaties. Expansion accords. Energy quotas. We achieved stability before existential competition could force uncontrolled growth.”

Representative Sen, from the fungal communes, shifted uneasily.

“Yes,” she said. “Obviously.”

Thir enlarged the graph further.

A single line, bright red, diverged violently from all others.

Humanity.

“They did not stabilise.”

The chamber remained silent.

Thir felt an unfamiliar sensation tightening his respiratory spiracles.

Not fear.

Anticipation.

“Their cooperative instincts are extraordinarily advanced,” he said. “Observe.”

The display shifted again.

Human disaster responses.

Volunteers entering flood zones. Medical workers remaining in plague regions despite lethal risk. Mass food distribution networks assembled by civilians. Individuals sacrificing themselves for genetically unrelated strangers at rates exceeding several Compact species' care-bond thresholds.

Then the image split.

On one side: humanitarian relief fleets.

On the other: strategic nuclear arsenals.

The delegates recoiled physically.

Kel flattened her fronds in alarm. “How did they survive atomic weapon development?”

“No species survives that phase without unification,” whispered Ool.

“They did not unify,” Thir replied.

That finally broke the chamber's composure.

Three delegates began speaking simultaneously before abruptly stopping themselves in embarrassment.

Thir continued before order protocols needed activation.

“They competed through the atomic age. They maintained ideological, economic, religious, and national divisions while possessing extinction-level arsenals.”

“That is irrational.”

“Yes.”

“You are implying sapience failure.”

“No.” Thir paused. “I am implying something worse.”

He expanded the timeline.

Wars.

Recoveries.

Economic booms.

Scientific revolutions.

More wars.

The pattern repeated with terrifying speed.

Human civilisation advanced during conflict, after conflict, because of conflict.

Rocket technology emerged from weapons programs.

Global communications from military infrastructure.

Early computation from cryptographic warfare.

Orbital launch systems derived directly from ballistic missile research.

Compact species had reached spaceflight through coordinated planetary initiatives spanning centuries.

Humanity had reached orbit because rival powers feared each other enough to spend impossible resources proving technological superiority.

“They turned competition into propulsion,” Thir said softly.

No one answered.

Because everyone in the chamber understood the implication at the same moment.

Every known pre-space civilisation that leaned too far toward competition destroyed itself.

Every civilisation that leaned too far toward cooperation remained planetbound indefinitely.

The Compact occupied the narrow survivable middle.

Humanity did not.

And yet Earth still lived.

Archivist Kel dimmed the projections.

“There is more,” she said.

The chamber lights darkened further as a final file opened.

Unlike the previous recordings, this one came from Compact archives.

Emergency signals.

Destroyed patrol craft.

Raided colonies.

Mass casualty reports.

The attackers identified by a single designation translated imperfectly into Compact trade language:

The Dominion.

The only known starfaring species more aggressive than humanity.

Thir watched the delegates tense reflexively.

Even after two centuries, the Dominion inspired instinctive dread.

Not because they conquered effectively. They did not. Their offensives were sporadic, undisciplined, often strategically pointless.

But because every interaction with them was soaked in cruelty.

Compact species fought reluctantly and ended wars quickly. The Dominion appeared to treat violence as recreation.

Yet even they remained fragmented, most of their strength consumed in endless internal warfare.

“They raid,” Kel said quietly. “They terrorise border systems. Then they vanish back into their own conflicts.”

“As always,” Sen muttered bitterly.

Kel rotated the display.

A new image appeared beside the Dominion vessels.

Human vessels.

Crude compared to Compact standards. Heat-heavy. Overarmed. Designed with alarming redundancy.

And accelerating outward from Earth at impossible speed.

The first extrasolar probes had already crossed into neighbouring darkspace currents.

No consensus petitions.

No Compact approval.

No growth accords.

Just momentum.

Ool's surface rippled with visible distress.

“They are entering the galactic community now?”

Kel gave a slow affirmative gesture.

“Estimated timeline to first contact?” asked Sen.

“Seven years,” Kel replied. “Possibly less.”

“And the recommendation?”

The Archivist hesitated.

That alone chilled the chamber more effectively than alarm.

Finally, Kel spoke.

“We do not have one.”

Silence again.

Thir studied the human ships on the display.

Individually, they were inefficient. Wasteful. Dangerous.

Collectively, they represented something the Compact had not seen in millennia.

A civilisation that still hungered.

Not merely for survival.

For more.

More territory. More knowledge. More wealth. More status. More meaning.

Humanity balanced on the edge of self-destruction every moment of its existence—

—and somehow transformed that tension into motion.

Thir realised, with growing unease, that the Compact had spent thousands of years assuming moderation was the apex of civilisation.

Perhaps moderation was merely survivability.

And perhaps survivability was not the same thing as destiny.

At the chamber's centre, Earth's image rotated slowly in the dark.

Blue.

Violent.

Alive.
